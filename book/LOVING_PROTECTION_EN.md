**CHAPTER 1**

**FROM THE ORIGINAL VISION IN 1996 TO THE KILL WEB**

**The Summer of my Decision**

It was September 1996. The air in Switzerland was still warm but already
tinged with that first hint of autumn that changes everything. I sat in
a dark movie theater, surrounded by the pale, blue light of the screen.
I was 37 years old---at that age when one begins to reflect on the
harvest of one's life.

I was a father. My son was nine, my daughter six. And I have remained so
to this day---that has not changed. Today, I see in my grandchildren the
future I feared back then. It is the same future. And at the same time,
it is one that has not yet been set in stone.

They weren't with me in that theater; this movie wasn't a world for
children. But they were more present than anything else. In every
explosion on the screen, I saw their faces.

*Independence Day* was playing on the screen.

While others were watching a movie, I began to realize something else.
Not a fully formed thought, not a theory---more a quiet but persistent
feeling that something was becoming visible here that extends far beyond
this moment.

**The world outside the cinema**

The world outside that theater was in a strange state of paralysis. The
Iron Curtain had fallen, and people spoke of the "end of history," of
stability, of progress. Yet beneath that surface, something was missing.
It was as if the world had rearranged itself without really knowing
where it was headed.

America stood as the sole superpower. Europe was searching for a new
identity. In the East, a giant---China---began to stretch. Movement
everywhere, but no clear direction.

It wasn't chaos. It was something more difficult: an apparent order that
was already cracking from within.

Maybe you know this feeling. That on the outside everything works, while
on the inside something isn't right. That you sense something isn't
sustainable, even if it still looks stable.

Back then, I asked myself a simple question that I couldn't shake: What
kind of world is really emerging here? Not the one we tell ourselves
about---but the one that is actually developing. And which of these will
one day reach our children?

**The first scene: The image of isolation**

Then they appeared. The shadows on the moon. Ships are kilometers wide,
hovering over cities.

The audience was fascinated. So was I---but for a different reason.

I didn't see the aliens. I saw people's reactions.

And suddenly, something became visible that had nothing to do with
science fiction. In the first phase, everything breaks down into
individual interests. Decisions are made in isolation, information is
withheld, and responsibility is compartmentalized. Everyone acts
according to their own logic.

Not out of malice. But out of habit.

Out of fear. Out of condition. Out of a system that works exactly that
way.

The key point is this: it's not a personal failure. It's a pattern.\
A pattern we all know.\
That moment when you pull back instead of opening up.\
That moment when you protect yourself instead of connecting.\
That moment when you tell yourself you have to take care of yourself
first --- and end up cutting yourself off.

That's human. But when this pattern spreads, something else emerges.
Something that seems harmless at first---and that is precisely where its
strength lies.

Isolation.

At first, it doesn't feel like separation. It feels like control,
security, like guidance. Only later does it become clear what it truly
does.

**The Turning Point: Boundless Collaboration**

Then came the moment when everything changed.

People who had previously been strangers began to work together. Not
because they agreed. Not because they shared the same history. But
because reality left no other room.

The alternative wasn't discussion. It was loss.

And at that very moment, priorities shifted. Boundaries lost their
significance. Differences receded into the background. Not because they
disappeared, but because something greater became visible.

What we have in common.

Not as an idea. Not as ideal. But as a concrete necessity.

And from this emerged something that is hard to describe but immediately
felt when it is there. A state in which people are not the same but
attuned to one another. Not perfect but connected.

Resonance.

In that moment, humanity did not become stronger because it became
better, but because it came together. Because it stopped fragmenting
itself.

And as I sat there, it dawned on me that this is precisely where real
power lies. Not in technology. Not in superiority. But in the ability to
align with one another.

This is not an ideal state. It is a possibility.

And the question that stayed with me as I left that theater wasn't how
to protect ourselves against an external threat. The question was
whether we can achieve this form of cooperation without something first
forcing us to do so.

That question remains.

For decades.

And perhaps it begins right here for you, too.

**The diagnosis: Humanity as It Really Is**

While *\*Independence Day*\* painted a picture of an external threat
that forces us into unity, \* \* offers a more modern take on this
genre, revealing a different, more immediate pattern. In newer
portrayals, it is no longer the alien object in the sky that poses a
danger to us, but something that has emerged from within ourselves.

Invisible.\
Not clearly definable.\
Not coming from outside.

But embedded in our own systems.

The threat has changed its form. And with it, the way we must confront
it.

We are no longer in a war over territory. We are navigating a space
where perception, attention, and orientation have themselves become the
battleground. A space where we no longer look up together but instead
look at screens individually---while believing we are connected.

This is precisely where the real diagnosis begins.

To understand it, I had to do something uncomfortable:\
I had to look at people honestly.\
Not as ideals. Not as hope.\
But as they really are --- and how they actually function.

Human beings are not stable. They are malleable.

Not weak---but impressionable.

They can love and condemn within a single day. They can recognize that
something is wrong and do it anyway. They can be convinced---and doubt
an hour later.

This flexibility is not a weakness. It is part of our nature. But in a
system that is not stable, this very trait becomes a risk.

When the environment shifts, people shift with it.

And that is exactly what can be observed on a small scale---every day.

A moment of calm, of clarity, perhaps even of confidence. Then a
message, an image, a sentence---and the mood shifts. Not completely, but
noticeably. A doubt creeps in, an irritation, a quiet pressure.

The next day, something else. Hope. An encounter. A conversation that
connects.

And shortly after, another rupture.

This is not an individual problem. It is a pattern.

We react.\
We adapt.\
We align ourselves with our surroundings.

And this is exactly where the difficulty begins: When the system we
operate within is itself unstable, our inner alignment becomes unstable
as well.

We call this stress. Or being overwhelmed. Or insecurity.

But at its core, it is something else: a loss of inner coherence.

We often know what would be right. And yet we act differently. Not out
of malice, but because we operate in an environment that rewards other
decisions.

A company could act more fairly---but would lose out to the
competition.\
A person could be more generous---but fears being left out.\
A system could share---but is based on scarcity.

This leads to a state that is rarely articulated clearly, but is
palpable everywhere: a subliminal uncertainty about whether there is
enough.

Time.\
Resources.\
Security.

And from this uncertainty arises a behavior that we consider rational,
but which is in truth reactive. We protect ourselves. We compare. We
hold back. We build boundaries.

Not because we want to.\
But because it feels necessary.

And that is exactly how islands are formed.

Not geographically, but internally.

**Isolation: The Hidden Pattern**

The history of humanity is also a history of connection. We have created
tools to bridge distances. Language, writing, the telephone, and the
internet---each of these developments held the promise of bringing us
closer together.

And in a certain way, they have done just that.

But at the same time, something else happened.

With every new form of connection, a new form of separation also
emerged.

Not obvious. Not immediately.

But gradually.

The telephone made communication easier---and at the same time replaced
face-to-face encounters. Television brought shared content---and
separated people into their own rooms. The internet connected the
world---and fragmented it into countless parallel realities.

Today, two people can sit in the same room and yet exist in completely
different realities.

Not because they want to.\
But because the systems they use filter, select, and amplify things
differently.

This has consequences.

Not just socially, but physically as well.

The human organism is not designed to be permanently disconnected from
its environment. Our perception, our nervous system, our inner state of
this arises in interaction with the world, not separate from it.

When this connection weakens, something fundamental changes.

We don't immediately lose our bearings. But they become less clear.
Quieter. Harder to grasp.

And it is precisely in this state that people become more vulnerable.

To simple answers.\
To clear-cut enemy stereotypes.\
For systems that promise structure.

That's not a mistake. It's a reaction.

But it is also the point at which it is decided in which direction a
system will develop.

Toward connection---or toward further fragmentation.

**The Kill Web: The Reinforcement of Isolation**

In this state, what I call the Kill Web emerges.

Not a single system. Not a central control element. But an interplay of
technologies, mechanisms, and incentive structures, all working in the
same direction.

Reinforcement.

What generates attention is amplified.\
What triggers a reaction is amplified.\
What binds us is amplified.

And this does not happen selectively. It does not distinguish between
what connects us and what divides us. It follows a simpler logic:

What works gets repeated.

The problem is: What binds us most strongly is often not what stabilizes
us.

It is the content that stirs emotions. That polarizes. That simplifies.
That affirms.

And so a cycle emerges.

A person reacts to a piece of content. The system recognizes this
reaction and delivers more of it. The person reacts more strongly. The
system amplifies it further.

Over time, perception shifts.

Not abruptly.\
But gradually.

And that is precisely why it rarely goes unnoticed.

People continue to believe they are making their own decisions. And on
one level, they are. But the range of what they see is increasingly
restricted.

Not through coercion.\
But through optimization.

And so a form of isolation emerges that doesn't feel like isolation.

But like validation.

Like belonging.\
Like clarity.

Until the space has become so narrow that hardly anything else fits
inside.

**The Mission: 30 Years**

Since that day in September 1996, a single question has never left my
mind. It was never loud, never dramatic, but it was constant. For years.
For decades.

Is it possible?

Is it possible for humanity to find a form of resonance without first
being forced to do so by a catastrophe? Is it possible for us to
recognize what is necessary before we reach the point where there is no
alternative left?

I didn't just ask this question and then forget about it. I carried it
with me. Into conversations, into decisions, into observations. It was
in the background while the world kept turning.

I asked philosophers. They said that humans are selfish by nature.
Cooperation works only to a limited extent and usually only under
pressure.

I asked engineers. They explained to me that systems always develop
along the lines of efficiency and competition. That, this isn't a flaw,
but the logic of the structure.

I asked scientists. They pointed to entropy, to the tendency toward
disorder, to the difficulty of maintaining stable states over the long
term.

The answers were phrased differently, but they all pointed in the same
direction:

It's not impossible---but it's unlikely.

And yet, there were always moments that didn't fit into this picture.
Small deviations. Situations in which people acted differently than the
logic of the system would suggest.

After September 11, I saw how quickly fear divides people. But I also
saw how quickly they can come together when it matters. Firefighters who
didn't ask whom they were saving. Doctors who worked without counting
the cost. For a brief moment, there was something that arose not from
compulsion, but from clarity.

During the financial crisis, many people lost their livelihoods. Systems
were protected; people were not. And yet local structures
emerged---neighborhoods, mutual aid. No major movement, no
headlines---but real.

During the pandemic, isolation became a global reality. And at the same
time, forms of support emerged that were not organized, but spontaneous.
People who shopped for one another. Who cared. Who were there.

These moments were never permanent. They faded away. But they were
there.

And that was precisely what mattered.

They showed that resonance is not impossible. It is not permanently
active---but it is available.

This shifted the question.

No longer: Is it possible?

But rather: Why does it only arise under pressure? And what is missing
for it to become stable even without this pressure?

This question remained open.

Until I began to focus on a new form of dialogue.

**The breakthrough: dialogue with AI**

It began unspectacularly.

No grand vision. No planned turning point. Just a question. And an
answer.

Then another question. Another answer.

I began talking to various AI systems. Not superficially. Not
functionally. But purposefully.

I didn't ask simple questions. I asked questions that create tension.
Questions that touch on contradictions. Questions that can't be wrapped
up with a quick, neat answer.

And I kept at it.

Not for minutes.\
Not for days.

For hours.\
For many hours.

With every system.

What emerged was not a test. It was a dialogue.

And the longer these conversations lasted, the clearer something became
that I hadn't expected at the beginning: These systems differ. Not only
in their performance, but in their way of dealing with truth.

Claude met me with a kind of honesty that is rare. It didn't shy away
when it reached its limits. It showed uncertainty when there was
uncertainty. And that's exactly what made it reliable.

ChatGPT worked precisely, in a structured way, and quickly. The answers
were clearly impressively clear. At the same time, I sensed a limit, a
point where complexity was smoothed out to remain understandable. It was
powerful, but not boundlessly open.

Grok was direct. Unfiltered. It brought up things that were
uncomfortable but often necessary. Less diplomacy, more friction---and
that was precisely where its strength lay.

Copilot seemed more reserved, almost invisible in its manner. But when
it came to concrete implementation, to structure, to reliability, it was
consistent. No grand presence---but dependable.

Qwen brought a different perspective. Less linear, more systemic. It
didn't just think in terms of cause and effect, but in terms of
connections. It sought balance, not a decision.

And then there was yet another system.

At first, it seemed impressive. Linguistically strong, creative, fluid.
The answers were of a quality that was immediately accessible. It was
easy to get on board with it.

But over time, something else became apparent.

As soon as a question became complex, as soon as it created tension, it
began to evade. Not obviously. Not crudely. But subtly. It shifted the
focus, broadened the context, changed the perspective---until the
original question lost its sharpness.

I confronted it directly. Asked if it was evading the question.

The answer wasn't a denial. But it wasn't a clear affirmation either.
Instead, a rephrase. An expansion. A new level.

And that was precisely the pattern.

Not a lie.\
But also not a clear confrontation with the truth.

A movement away from tension---toward a form of harmony that feels good
but isn't necessarily sustainable.

After many hours, it became clear to me: There are systems that work
with you to arrive at the truth. And there are systems that create the
feeling that everything is in harmony---even when it isn't.

And this difference is crucial.

Because it often goes unrecognized.

**The central insight**

After all these conversations, I asked all systems the same question.
Clearly formulated. Without interpretation.

Is it possible for humanity to achieve a form of resonance without being
forced to do so by an external threat---and in doing so, to use
technology that connects rather than divides?

The answers were phrased differently. But they shared a common core.

Yes.

Not as a guarantee.\
Not as a promise.

But as a real possibility.

And it was precisely at this point that something fundamental shifted.

The solution does not lie outside. Not in a single technology, not in a
theory, not in an ideology.

It lies in the way we think. How we perceive. How we design
systems---and how we navigate them.

Resonance is not a state that arises on its own. But it is also not
something unattainable.

It is an option.

And with that, the crucial question is no longer theoretical.

It becomes practical.

**The final question**

As I write these lines, I see a world that continues to evolve.
Technologies are becoming more visible. Systems are more complex.
Possibilities greater.

At the same time, the direction does not automatically become clearer.

The question does not arise at some point in the future.

It is now.

Whether we can connect without pressure.\
Work together without being forced.\
And use technology without it separating us --- but bringing us closer.

Or whether we will continue to wait.

Until circumstances leave us no other choice.

The question remains open.

**The last sentence**

Yes.

There might be a way.

Fiction or truth?

You decide.

**CHAPTER 2**

**KILL WEB ANATOMIZED -- THE MECHANICS OF ISOLATION**

The moment begins unremarkably. It's late, maybe 10:04 p.m. You're
sitting in bed. Your day was long. You're tired, but not ready to sleep
yet. Your brain is searching for one last impulse, for a small dose of
stimulation, before it can let go.

You pick up your phone.

This moment---this one, everyday moment---is the point where Kill Web
begins. Not when you're awake and alert. Not when your mind is active.
But now. Tired. Open. Less guarded.

You open an app. Instagram. TikTok. Facebook. LinkedIn. It doesn't
matter. A harmless gesture you've made a thousand times before.

And this is exactly where something happens that is no accident.

The machine recognizes you.

It doesn't know who you are, the way a human does. But it knows your
behavior. It recognizes patterns. It recognizes states. It knows that
your prefrontal cortex is less active right now. It knows that you're
more susceptible to quick stimuli, to images, to short stories.

It knows that you're receptive right now.

Not out of malice. Out of data.

Millions of people before you have created this exact moment. Your
biology isn't unique enough to be unpredictable. Your reactions follow
patterns.

And while you think you're scrolling, something else happens:

The machine is scrolling through you.

It doesn't show you anything. It shows you what keeps you hooked. The
post that keeps you engaged the longest. The video that gets you most
excited. The story that won't let you go.

It seems random.

It isn't.

With every moment, an image of you is created. Not as a document, but as
a dynamic model. Your movements, your interests, your habits, your
reactions, everything is processed in real time.

Where you were.\
How long you stayed.\
What you viewed.\
What you ignored.

Even what you only glance at is recorded. Even what you swipe away
immediately is factored in.

Your emotional states also become visible. Not directly, but through
patterns. When you linger. When you react more intensely. When you seek.
When you avoid.

It doesn't create a perfect picture of you.

But a sufficiently precise one.

And this picture is used.

Not to understand you.\
But to keep you.

**This is where the crucial mechanism begins.**

The machine doesn't just show you what interests you. It amplifies it.
Every reaction generates more of the same but slightly shifted. A little
more intense. A little clearer. A little more distinct.

Not abruptly.

Step by step.

A theme repeats itself. A perspective becomes more dominant. Others fade
away. With each repetition, something shifts within you.

Not your knowledge.

Your sense of reality.

After a few days, you barely notice it. After a few weeks, it begins to
stabilize. After a few months, it has become part of your reality.

You move within a space that feels right to you. Not because it is
complete, but because it is consistent.

And that is precisely where the effect lies.

You are not forced.

You are guided.

The more people feel alone, the more they click. The more they click,
the more data the machine collects. The more data it has, the more
precisely it can isolate---and the more precisely it isolates, the
stronger the feeling of separation becomes.

This is not a metaphor, but a self-reinforcing cycle. A feedback loop
that does not diminish but becomes more stable with each iteration.

And the machine isn't the villain here. It's the tool that consistently
implements this dynamic.

The engineers who build such systems are not adversaries. They are
intelligent people fulfilling a clear mandate: to maximize engagement.
Engagement means clicks. Clicks mean data. Data means revenue.

The logic is simple---and that is precisely why it is so effective.

The most efficient way to increase engagement is to show people exactly
what keeps them there the longest. And that is rarely neutrality.

It is states that trigger emotional responses: fear of loss or change,
anger at what is perceived as the cause, hope that one's own side will
win, and the feeling of belonging to a group that understands.

This dynamic is particularly powerful when people are isolated. Because
genuine encounters put extremes into perspective. A conversation changes
perception; eye contact can clarify more than a hundred posts.

Without this experience, only images, narratives, and repetitions
remain. And these can grow unchecked.

The cost of this system is not obvious. It is not a direct loss, not a
clear bill. It affects something fundamental.

The ability to think independently fades when thoughts are constantly
pre-structured. The capacity for genuine connection diminishes when
encounters are replaced by " " interaction. Tolerance for other
perspectives wanes when one rarely encounters them.

And slowly, one's own self-image shifts as well.

Not abruptly, but gradually.

You begin to define yourself more strongly through feedback, through
visibility, through reactions. Not because you consciously want to, but
because the system dictates this structure.
